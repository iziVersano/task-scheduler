## 1. The Shared Responsibility Model: Boundaries of Responsibility in the Cloud

**Estimated Time: approx. 50 minutes**

The Shared Responsibility Model is a cornerstone of understanding cloud security. For advanced participants, however, it's not just about knowing the principles, but also applying them to complex, real-world scenarios and analyzing their implications precisely.

### Task Description:

1.  **In-depth Analysis of Complex Scenarios:**
    *   Choose a specific cloud service type (e.g., IaaS, PaaS, or SaaS).
    *   Describe a hypothetical scenario in which a security vulnerability has occurred (e.g., data loss, unauthorized access).
    *   Analyze in detail how the Shared Responsibility Model applies in this specific scenario. Clearly identify which aspects of the security vulnerability fall under the responsibility of the cloud provider and which under the customer's. Also, consider potential gray areas or areas where demarcation might be difficult.
    *   Discuss which technical controls the cloud provider makes available and which the customer *must* implement to fulfill their responsibility and minimize risk.

2.  **Compliance and Audit Implications:**
    *   Imagine your company is subject to strict compliance requirements (e.g., GDPR, HIPAA, or SOC 2).
    *   Explain how the Shared Responsibility Model influences the execution of compliance audits in a cloud environment.
    *   Describe which documents or proofs the cloud customer would typically request from the cloud provider to demonstrate their own compliance, and what role the customer themselves plays in providing evidence.

## 2. Infrastructure as a Service (IaaS): The Flexible Foundation

**Estimated Time: approx. 40 minutes**

IaaS offers the greatest flexibility in the cloud but also entails a high degree of responsibility for the user. For advanced participants, it is crucial to understand the strategic implications and architectural decisions that come with IaaS.

### Task Description:

1.  **IaaS vs. On-Premise Comparison (TCO and Operating Model):**
    *   Critically analyze the Total Cost of Ownership (TCO) for a typical enterprise application, operated either entirely in an IaaS environment or traditionally on-premise. Consider not only obvious costs (hardware, software licenses) but also indirect costs such as operation, maintenance, personnel, scalability, and resilience.
    *   Discuss the significant differences in the operating model and the required skillsets of the IT department between an IaaS and an on-premise strategy.

2.  **Architectural Challenges and Best Practices:**
    *   You are the lead architect of a company that wants to migrate an existing, monolithic application to an IaaS environment.
    *   Identify and describe at least three central architectural challenges that may arise during this migration, specifically for IaaS environments (e.g., network design, storage, database migration, high availability).
    *   For each challenge, formulate a concrete best practice or solution strategy that leverages the benefits of IaaS and minimizes risks.

## 3. Platform as a Service (PaaS): The Developer's Highway

**Estimated Time: approx. 40 minutes**

PaaS promises significant acceleration in development and deployment but also harbors specific challenges and limitations. An advanced understanding requires a critical examination of these aspects.

### Task Description:

1.  **Vendor Lock-in and Portability:**
    *   Discuss the concept of "vendor lock-in" in the context of PaaS solutions.
    *   Detail the various forms in which vendor lock-in can manifest (e.g., API dependencies, proprietary services, database integration).
    *   Develop strategies and architectural approaches to mitigate the risk of vendor lock-in when using PaaS services, without fully sacrificing the benefits of PaaS. Consider open-source approaches, containerization, or multi-cloud strategies.

2.  **PaaS in Microservices Architectures:**
    *   Analyze the suitability of PaaS platforms for the deployment and operation of microservices architectures.
    *   Identify and explain two significant advantages and two significant disadvantages of using PaaS for microservices compared to a pure IaaS or Container-as-a-Service (CaaS) solution.
    *   Provide recommendations on under what circumstances a company should prefer PaaS for its microservices strategy or rather refrain from it.

## 4. Software as a Service (SaaS): The Ready-Made Solution

**Estimated Time: approx. 30 minutes**

SaaS is ubiquitous, yet the deeper implications for businesses, especially concerning security, integration, and governance, are often more complex than they first appear.

### Task Description:

1.  **Security and Governance Challenges in Enterprise SaaS Usage:**
    *   Imagine a large enterprise uses a variety of SaaS applications (e.g., CRM, ERP, HR software, communication tools).
    *   Analyze at least three critical security challenges and two governance challenges that arise from this extensive SaaS usage (e.g., data sovereignty, identity and access management, shadow IT, auditability, compliance).
    *   Describe concrete measures and strategies that a company can implement to effectively manage these challenges and ensure the security and compliance of its SaaS landscape.

2.  **SLA, Performance, and Integration:**
    *   Discuss the importance of Service Level Agreements (SLAs) for SaaS applications from both a business and technical perspective. Which aspects are particularly critical here?
    *   Analyze how the performance of a SaaS application can be influenced by factors beyond the provider's control (e.g., customer's network, end devices) and what implications this has for user experience.
    *   Explain the complexity of integrating multiple SaaS applications with each other and with existing on-premise systems. Which integration techniques and patterns are typical here?
